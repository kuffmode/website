Short answer:*with great power comes great responsibilities, which we tend to forget. I simply couldn’t handle those responsibilities.*

![Image](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1200/0*YqnLXRMJVrcxLOMC.jpg)

Now here’s the long answer:

I studied Clinical Psychology for my BSc, then moved to Neuroscience, and now Computational Neuroscience. I have a few reasons why I jumped around like being interested in knowing how the brain makes behaviours and how to replicate it i*n silico.*For now, let’s stick to one reason why I gave up having a prestigious office: **responsibility.**

But let’s quickly roll back to when I started psychology, to see why I did that anyway. I wanted to learn*why we do what we do*. I was searching for reasons, explanations, causes, and well, I still do but back then I had no clue about “neuroscience”. I knew Sigmund Freud, Psychoanalysis, unconsciousness, ego, and some stuff about mental disorders. I liked them all, I thought that if I know the disordered mind then I can understand the “ordered” one and Voila! I’ll know why we do what we do.

To be honest, psychoanalysis is pretty powerful when it comes to explanations, of course, you dissect behaviours and thoughts and figure out hidden causes so I was so satisfied with the way I was going and I was good at it. Good enough that my professors were recommending me to go abroad and be a psychoanalyst because they could see the*spirit*in me. Cool, I killed the spirit in my last year when things started to get practical. I had to have an internship in a mental hospital, then I had courses on differential diagnosis, clinical interviewing and other hands-on stuff. I realized that I can’t handle the responsibility. Yes, one single responsibility, the future of my “patient”. My classmates were rushing to have clinical experiences, to talk with and consult people, to comfort them and release them from their mental dreads. But I just freaked out and stood there. What if I do something wrong in my fancy glamorous office? what if I say something that eventually puts my patient into more trouble? how the hell can I be really sure about what I’m doing is absolutely helpful? What if I say something that sounds professional to me but then a few days later I hear that my patient killed herself? Easy, she was depressed and I did my best to help her but hell no this wouldn’t work for me.

I was thinking about these, wanting to help people, know what’s wrong in their minds but also couldn’t accept the responsibility of their futures and **BAM**! I experienced the problem myself. I faced something stressful, I was losing my shit over it and I didn’t know how to handle my life so I sought help. I went to talk to my professors because I believed in their knowledge. Probably they tried their bests but it didn’t work at all, everything was collapsing for me and none of those heroes of mine could help, I could see that they are even incapable of understanding what is wrong but I followed their suggestions and spent most of my income, explored different approaches from my almighty psychoanalysis to beloved Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and new-age Positive psychology.

What helped me at the end was, sorry to say that, none of them but time. I could handle my emotional turbulence and intrusive thoughts because of a bunch of pills and I simply let them sink over time. After about 6 years, I know that I can’t do the same with someone else. I followed my therapists in social media and I know they are proud of themselves, promoting their success by giving talks and having big workshops. People have faith in them as I did and actually, I know some of my friends also faced problems and solved them there. So they are not seriously awful but I realised I can’t sample my patients to measure my success. Even if one therapy session ends up destructive, I failed. It’s that simple. This sounds stupid I know but we are talking about **people’s lives**, do you want to be that one who goes to a therapy room helpless and comes out hopeless? This psychologist you just visited had the success rate of %99 but that %1, in my opinion, outweighs. Having your %1 committing suicide is actually might not be the worst outcome of you failing as the therapist. That’s, of course, horrible but what if your patient ends up being a terrible partner and parent, leading to make a crooked generation. Can we accept our responsibility as being at least a part of many people’s suffering because we failed to help one? I gave up psychology because I couldn’t. I think psychotherapists are extremely brave and responsible or simply unaware. Yes, there are ignorant ones but let’s put them away. My personal experience didn’t suggest that they do really care because none of them followed me up to see why I stopped showing up. I assumed they are “busy and unaware”. I said it’s their job and I’m the one who should follow them but anyway, n=1 (although I argued how critical that is) can’t be a reliable source here. Especially because I underwent therapies in Iran, I don’t know about other countries and how they educate psychotherapists but as a general ethical point,**I think psychotherapists should be aware of their interventions and the long term consequences of every single therapy session**. It’s not always people crying out of joy saying you changed their lives. You, as a psychotherapist, might have changed other lives without knowing it because you destroyed them and never saw those people coming back. Can you accept what you have done there? I gave up on being a psychotherapist because I freaked out over this responsibility.

**Take home message for interested readers**, psychotherapy is seriously hard so your psychologist probably knows what she’s doing but in the end, you should also know what you are doing there. Sometimes psychotherapy is not **the** answer and you need more, sometimes it’s just the wrong approach, sorry to say but I studied the mess and I couldn’t figure out which approach could help me the most so I tried few of them. Don’t give up early, maybe you also need to try different approaches and give yourself time. And again, I’m talking about my personal experience as someone who tried both sides of the desk, which might be pretty different. Beware.

**Take home message for psychotherapists,** know exactly what you’re doing, predict, and handle the consequences. I know you can’t help everybody but you should also remind this to yourselves sometimes. Don’t be a stubborn outdated diagnosis machine because there’ll be better ones soon, be open to different approaches and know the limits of yours. If you think you can’t help, probably the best thing is to stop right there.

Good luck killing those madness monsters.
